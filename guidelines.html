Patent Instructions Guide

All you need to know about patent prosecution in India

Introduction

This guide provides a comprehensive understanding of the patent process in India, detailing key steps such as filing, examination, opposition, and the grant of patents, as followed by the Indian Patent Office (IPO).

Key Definitions

  • Appropriate Office: The designated IPO office based on the applicant's location.
  • Assignee: A person or entity to whom rights of the invention are assigned.
  • Invention: A new product or process involving an inventive step and industrial applicability.
  • Startup: An entity recognized under the Startup India initiative.

Filing Patent Application

There are various types of patent applications that can be filed, including:

  • Ordinary Applications
  • Convention Applications
  • PCT National Phase Applications
  • Divisional Applications
  • Patent of Addition

Applications can be filed electronically through the IPO's e-filing system, which supports digital signatures and online payments.

Publication of Application

Patent applications are published in the official e-journal 18 months from the filing or priority date unless secrecy directions apply. Early publication is available upon request.

Provisional and Complete Specification

Provisional Specification: A preliminary version allowing the applicant to secure a priority date. It includes a title and description but no claims.

Complete Specification: A detailed document that must be filed within 12 months of the provisional application, including a full description, the best method of performance, and the claims.

Divisional Application and Patent of Addition

Divisional applications are filed when a patent contains more than one invention. A Patent of Addition is filed when an improvement or modification is made to an existing patent.

Examination and Grant

After publication, patent applications are subject to formal and substantive examination. If the examiner raises objections, the applicant must address them before the patent can be granted.

Post-Grant Opposition

After a patent is granted, any person can file an opposition within a year, citing issues like lack of novelty or insufficient disclosure.

Appeals

Decisions of the IPO (such as refusal to grant a patent) can be appealed to the Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB).

Revocation of Patent

Patents can be revoked by the IPO or courts under circumstances like non-disclosure or if the invention lacks novelty or inventive step.

Compulsory Licensing

Compulsory licenses are granted if the patented invention is not reasonably available to the public or if public needs are not being met.

Patent Agents

Patent agents are registered professionals who represent applicants before the IPO. They must meet certain qualifications and registration requirements.

Offences and Penalties

Penalties apply for offenses such as falsifying patent documents or unauthorized practice by patent agents.
